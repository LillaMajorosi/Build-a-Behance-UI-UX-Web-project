Museum
history
The Birmingham Museum began as a private exhibition hall in the early 19th
century. At first it was one room in the free library, in which several dozen
photographs and copper statue of Buddha from the 1st century BC were
hung. Gradually, the museum grew, donated and acquired paintings, artifacts,
various collections, they occupied more and more premises in different parts
of the city, and in 1885 the Prince of Wales solemmly opened a new biulding
specifically for the museum and art gallery. The building was built in the
Victorian Baroque style.
Above the museum and art gallery is a clock that the locals call Big Broom (brum is the
local term for the name of the city. inhabitants and dialect). The clock was donated to the
city by a researcher who measured meteorological and chronological data in Birmingham.
The chime of the Birmingham clock is very similar to the chime of London's Big Ben, hence
its name.

The art gallery has a large collection of Pre-Raphaelite paintings, and there are more
paintings by Edward Burne-Jones than in other museums in the world. A large collection of
English artists from different periods. There are Flemings, and French, and Italians, and
Spaniards (Rubens, Botticelli, Murillo).
The museum belongs to the city council. Entrance to the museum and gallery is free.
