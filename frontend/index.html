Museum
history
The Birmingham Museum began as a private exhibition hall in the early 19th
century. At first it was one room in the free library, in which several dozen
photographs and copper statue of Buddha from the 1st century BC were
hung. Gradually, the museum grew, donated and acquired paintings, artifacts,
various collections, they occupied more and more premises in different parts
of the city, and in 1885 the Prince of Wales solemmly opened a new biulding
specifically for the museum and art gallery. The building was built in the
Victorian Baroque style.
Above the museum and art gallery is a clock that the locals call Big Broom (brum is the
local term for the name of the city. inhabitants and dialect). The clock was donated to the
city by a researcher who measured meteorological and chronological data in Birmingham.
The chime of the Birmingham clock is very similar to the chime of London's Big Ben, hence
its name.

The art gallery has a large collection of Pre-Raphaelite paintings, and there are more
paintings by Edward Burne-Jones than in other museums in the world. A large collection of
English artists from different periods. There are Flemings, and French, and Italians, and
Spaniards (Rubens, Botticelli, Murillo).
The museum belongs to the city council. Entrance to the museum and gallery is free.

In pursuit
of beauty
The memory of the wedding of Thetis (either a
sea nymph, or the daughter of the centaur
Chiron) and Peleus, a mere mortal, has been
preserved for centuries. No one before or since
has seem such a significant feast, which was
attended by almost all the Olympian gods.

And the story that ended with the wedding began
long before that. Zeus, a great lover of women,
was inflamed with passion (for the umpteenth
time) for the pupil of his wife Hera, the young
and beautyful Thetis.
The Feast of Peleus. Artist: Sir Edward Burne-Jones
And he would have taken possession of it, if not for the prediction of Promotheus that Thetis
would give birth to a son who would be stronger than his father. It was not at all part of Zeus's
plans for someone to overthrow him from the Olympic throne. And he decided to arrange the
wedding of Thetis with a mortal. This child will definitely not claim the Olympic throne.

The choice of Zeus fell on Peleis. Although Peleus was a hero who followed the Golden
Fleece with the Argonauts and participated in the battle for Troy, he was still only a mortal.
And in order to intermarry with the gods, Peleis had to defeat Thetis in a duel.
And only then take her as his wife.

The wedding of Thetis and Peleus was celebrated in the cave of Chiron on Mount Pelion in
the presence of all the Olympian gods. Well, almost everyone. One Eris, the goddess of
discord, was not invited to the wedding. By the way, this decision ended tragically for both
gods and people - the Trojan War began. But that's another story.
Bloomart Abraham (1564-1651) - The wedding of Peleus and Thetis
